Problem

After my first few months with a regional economic development (ED) organization, it was clear that the issue facing those in the ED world was far larger than just “landing the next big business.” In order to make that happen, there are numerous other factors in play: sites and buildings, utilities, incentives, and workforce.

Workforce is an issue that seems to plague both business retention as well as business expansion. A business is not going to set up shop in an area where they will not be able to attract a full workforce. In addition, it is difficult for smaller businesses to expand, or sometimes even stay afloat, if their workforce is insufficient.

Investigation

After hearing about workforce being a significant issue for many of the businesses within my region, I decided to take a closer look into where the gap may be. My work gave me access to Emsi, a Labor Market Analytics service. That coupled with a few downloads from the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) and I was ready for some investigation.

Talent Gap Analyses are basically an analysis between supply and demand. The supply data came in the form of completion data for all accredited post-secondary institutions in the region. This is available for download from the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES). The demand data came from job postings. My workplace gave me access to Emsi, a Labor Market Analytics service, which happened to have that report available. To connect the two datasets, crosswalks exist that map educational programs to the occupations they typically fill. You can then explore what kind of jobs for which you are over- and under-supplied. For economic development, this helps you figure out which companies would be good targets because your region will be able to supply the workforce. On the other hand, it can also tell you the areas you may need to talk to local institutions about to help start a talent pipeline to attract a different set of businesses.

The job postings data showed Heavy and Tractor-Trailer Truck Drivers as the top in-demand job. As I looked down the list I saw similar items: Light Truck Drivers, Supervisors of Material Moving Workers, Driver/Sales Workers, etc. The high demand over time made me curious as to whether there was a gap in supply for my area.

Visualizing the Data

ArcGIS Pro is an incredible tool for geospatial analysis. It allows joining of spreadsheets to location layers which allowed the image at the top of this post to be created. Mapping the job postings showed that there is a massive need for truck drivers in our eastern-most county. That one county blows all others out of the water.

As a note, I do know that Jefferson County is a bit of an outlier for economic development. JSW Steel moved in and there have been on-going talks about an ethane cracker nearby. Not to mention the fact that the shale underlying that region is part of the most productive in the nation.

Overall, the page's header image shows that there are institutions in neighboring areas that are producing far more truck drivers than we are. Since each of those areas have major metropolitan areas, they’re going to have far more job opportunities period. It will be difficult to pull a worker from that market as their rate of pay is typically higher. In order to combat this particular issue, our employers will either need to focus on attracting talent from the areas producing it, or we need to work with local educational institutions within the region to offer programs to produce truck drivers closer to home. In any case, this gives our team some further background into what is happening for this particular occupation as well as a process for us to investigate other talent pipelines in the region.
